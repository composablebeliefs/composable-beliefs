# 1 · The epistemic core

Every node in a belief graph is one of exactly four kinds, and which one it is tells you what it claims, what it must be grounded in, and what would make it wrong. This chapter teaches the four types, the licensing idea that powers the hardest distinction between them, the immutability discipline that makes change traceable, and the field the schema refuses to carry. Get these straight and the rest of the schema falls out of them.

## One structural type per epistemic operation

The framework starts from a small claim about what an agent ever does with a piece of knowledge. There are four distinct moves: you can **attest** what a source said, **aggregate** several claims into one, **infer** a conclusion that goes past what you were told, or **prescribe** something that ought to hold. The schema gives each move its own structural type, and contract `cb:c051` makes it law: the `type` field accepts exactly four values - `attestation`, `aggregation`, `inference`, `prescription` - one per epistemic operation, and *determines which other fields are meaningful*.

That last phrase is the load-bearing one. The type is a function over the rest of the record. It decides whether `deps` are required, whether an `artifact` must be present, whether the belief may ever be materialized into work, and what the verifier checks when it asks whether the node is well-formed.

| Type | Operation | What it asserts | Grounding requirement | Fails when |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `attestation` | attest | one atomic statement of what a single source said | `artifact` + dated evidence; no deps | an immutable record; superseded only if the source is re-read differently |
| `aggregation` | aggregate | exactly what its deps jointly state | deps; subjects stay inside the deps' subjects | one of its deps fails (the deduction guarantee breaks) |
| `inference` | infer | a conclusion licensed to exceed what its deps jointly state | deps | falsified on its own while its deps stay true |
| `prescription` | prescribe | something that should happen or must hold | deps or a stipulation artifact; contracts may stand alone | violated, or withdrawn |

Two columns split the four into pairs. "What it asserts" separates the three descriptive types, which report on the world, from the single prescriptive type, which tells the world what to do. "Fails when" draws the same line from the other side: descriptions can be falsified; prescriptions can only be violated or withdrawn.

```
                    the type enum (cb:c051)
                             |
          +------------------+------------------+
          |                                     |
     DESCRIPTIVE                           PRESCRIPTIVE
   (mind fits world)                     (world fits mind)
          |                                     |
   +------+-----------+                         |
   |          |       |                         |
attestation aggregation inference          prescription
  attest    aggregate    infer               prescribe
   |          |            |                    |
  one       conjoin    generalize /        rule, policy, plan,
  source    the deps   abduce beyond       contract-grade spec
                       the deps
```

> **A note on names.** The types were renamed on 2026-07-01 from `primitive`/`compound`/`inference`/`directive` to the nominalizations of the four verbs - a vocabulary migration, not a schema change (evidence 3 on `cb:c051`). Immutable claims authored before the rename still use the old words; the code reads both for the compat epoch. This guide uses the current names throughout.

## Attestation: what one source said

An attestation is one atomic statement of what a single source said. It carries an `artifact` (a typed URI naming the source) and one or more dated evidence entries, and it has no deps, because it rests on a source rather than on other beliefs. An attestation is the only place new information enters the graph; everything else is built out of attestations by composition.

"Atomic" is easy to misread. Atomicity is about provenance, not logical simplicity (`cb:a475`, the atomicity doctrine): what makes a claim an attestation is that it derives from *one source*, not that it is short or single-clause. The doctrine is explicit that a claim conjoining separable assertions from different reads is a mis-authored aggregation and is split at authoring time. If a review note says two things that came from two different reads, those are two attestations, even though they arrived in one sentence.

Here is a real one - the belief this repository's own freshness discipline rests on:

```
$ mix bs show cb:a386

Type:      attestation
Kind:      observation
Claim:     A digest file that caches active implications, regenerated by a
           command, has freshness that depends on procedural enforcement ...
           Persisted caches of graph-derived content whose freshness is
           procedural rather than structural are antipatterns ...
Artifact:  user:review-session-2026-04-21
Evidence:  In one session, several implications were added and the digest was
           not regenerated; subsequent readers would see the pre-change state ...
           (date: 2026-04-21)
Support:   artifacts=1 evidence=1 deps=0
```

The claim is the generalization the author drew; the evidence is the specific narrative of what happened in that one session. The space between the two is where a later reader audits whether the claim actually follows from what the source said. Note `deps=0`: an attestation answers to its source, to nothing upstream.

## Aggregation: exactly what the deps say

An aggregation states exactly what its deps jointly state, and nothing more. It is the one truth-preserving deduction the schema bothers to store. The restraint is deliberate (`cb:a473`): a deduction's conclusion is contained in its premises, and deductive consequences are infinite and free - once the graph holds A and B, "A and B" follows mechanically, and so does every other consequence. A query tool can compute those on demand; there is no reason to persist them. The one entailment the schema stores is conjunction introduction, because there the epistemic work is not the deduction, which is trivial, but the **selection**: choosing which claims to assemble and naming the agreement they make together. "Two independent scorers reached the same outcome" is a claim neither scorer's report makes alone, yet it is fully contained in what they say jointly.

The strict-aggregate doctrine (`cb:a474`) protects that boundary from the authoring side: an aggregation's claim is the conjunction, with any interpretation or generalization trimmed off and pushed up into an inference. The formal guard is [subject containment](../glossary.md#subject-containment) (`cb:c058`): an active aggregation's `subjects` - the things it is about - must be a subset of the union of its deps' subjects, because a conjunction cannot be about something its parts are not about. The moment a claim's subjects escape its deps' union, it has stopped aggregating and started inferring; that is the machine-checkable line between the two types.

> **Caveat.** The framework's own `cb:` graph currently contains zero aggregations (`mix bs stats`). The type is fully real and contract-governed, but it earns its keep where many beliefs share subjects so that containment has something to bite on - which happens in eval collections, where six-subject observations pile up. [Chapter 7](7-eval-ledger.md) teaches the aggregation from a real eval finding.

## Inference: licensed to exceed the evidence

An inference is a conclusion the graph lets you draw even though it outruns what its deps jointly state. Where an aggregation stays inside its premises, an inference makes an [ampliative](../glossary.md#ampliative) move - a generalization from cases, or an abduction to the best explanation. The claim genuinely says more than the deps; that is the whole point of recording it, and the reason it can be wrong on its own terms.

Make it concrete with the illustration the framework uses for its own eval ledger. Two independent scorers each look at the same model output and each report the same atomic fact: on case 7 of run 3, record number 7 was omitted from a bulk write of twelve records. Those are two attestations. Now an author concludes, and stores as an inference: "this model at this snapshot silently drops records from bulk writes larger than ten items."

```
attestation  "run 3 / case 7: record #7 dropped from a 12-record write"
attestation  "run 3 / case 7: record #7 dropped from a 12-record write"
        \                                  /
         \             deps              /
          v                             v
inference  "this model at this snapshot silently drops records
            from bulk writes larger than ten items"

   scope widens: one observed case -> every such write, ever
   no deductive chain bridges that gap (the problem of induction)
```

Look at the gap. The premises are about one write on one case; the conclusion is about every such write the model will ever perform. No sequence of deductive steps gets from the first to the second, because a finite set of observations never entails a universal claim - the problem of induction, unbridgeable in principle. The inference type exists precisely to hold a claim that has stepped over that gap *on purpose*, so the graph can register that someone concluded it and can later hold the conclusion accountable.

**The price of the licence is independent falsifiability.** Run the same eval against a newer snapshot and the bulk writes come back complete. That clean run does not touch the original observations - record 7 really was dropped on that day, and those attestations stay active and true forever, because they only ever claimed what one scorer saw at one moment. What the clean run kills is the generalization: the inference is superseded by a successor scoped to the new snapshot. Observations are immutable measurements; the inference is the perishable claim built on top of them. That asymmetry is the whole reason the two operations get different types, and it is why the supersession lifecycle below exists.

> **Why "inference" and not "implication".** The type was once called `implication`, and the rename is argued in `cb:a473`: an implication, by definition, is contained in its premises; a node whose claim is licensed to *exceed* its premises cannot honestly wear that name. There is a structural reason too - the contract rule-kind `implies` already exists on the prescriptive side of the schema (chapter 2), and reusing the word for a descriptive type would put one lexeme on both sides of the is/ought boundary.

**The verifier reads the relation, never the author.** "Licensed to exceed its premises" sounds like a statement about intent, and intent is not something a verifier can read. So the schema works with the one observable trace the ampliative move always leaves: scope. Per `cb:a472`, type is a near-function of three checkable properties - `type = f(mood(kind), grounding(artifact/deps), scope(subjects))`. Mood (does the kind describe or prescribe?) separates prescription from the rest; grounding (artifact or deps?) separates attestation from the derived types; scope (do the subjects stay inside the deps' union?) separates aggregation from inference. The author's mental act is out of scope by construction - an author can feel like they are deducing while generalizing, which is exactly why the check interrogates the record instead of the reasoning.

> **Pitfall.** The most common mis-author is an aggregation that quietly generalizes: you assemble two observations and, while naming the agreement, sharpen the wording until the claim is about the model in general rather than the two cases the deps cover. The subjects have escaped the deps' union, so it is an inference, and it now needs the falsification lifecycle an aggregation never carries. The fix is `cb:a474`: keep the aggregation's claim exactly the conjunction, and lift the generalization into a separate inference that deps on it. Two honest nodes beat one node that is secretly two.

> **Caveat.** Subject containment is necessary, not sufficient. A claim whose subjects stay inside its deps' union could be a faithful aggregation, or a synthesizing inference that happens not to widen scope. `cb:a472` names this "the residual gray zone" and hands it to the write flow rather than the verifier: the check rules out one class of error; human judgment at authoring time owns the residue.

## Prescription: what should hold

A prescription says something should happen or must hold: rules, conventions, policies, plans, and machine-checkable specifications. It is the only type the world can *disobey*, and so it is the only type the framework attaches its action machinery to: only prescriptions are [materialized](../glossary.md#materialization) into concrete work items, only prescriptions are audited for conflicts, and only prescriptions can be graded up into [contracts](../glossary.md#contract).

The boundary between inference and prescription is direction of fit, made operational by `cb:a471`: **ask what would count as the belief being wrong.**

| If the belief can be... | then it is... | direction of fit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| falsified by the world | an `inference` | mind fits world |
| violated by the world | a `prescription` | world fits mind |
| withdrawn by its author | a `prescription` | world fits mind |

An inference is falsified: the world disagrees, the belief is defective, and it is superseded or retracted. A prescription is violated - the world disobeys, and the response is to flag the violation, never to revise the rule - or withdrawn: the house stops standing behind it. The v1 schema had a single type that conflated the two moods, and the machinery was already sorting by the hidden boundary - materialization, the conflict audit, and contract grading attach only to prescriptions, while staleness and supersession-on-evidence respond to falsification (`cb:a470`). Splitting the type made the line the code was drawing into a thing the schema states out loud.

Grounding also works differently on the prescriptive side. A descriptive belief answers to a source or to other beliefs; a prescription answers to an *adoption*. So prescription grounding (`cb:c059`) lets a non-contract prescription rest on deps or on a **stipulation artifact** - a `plan:`, `user:`, `session:`, or `document:` URI recording where the rule was adopted - so a convention does not have to manufacture fake deps to look grounded. A prescription formalized to contract grade (non-empty `rules` or `invariants`) may even stand alone, because the rules are their own justification. Contract is a structural *grade* of a prescription, never a fifth type; that story is [chapter 2](2-schema.md#contracts-prescriptions-with-teeth).

## One real belief of each type

Every belief below is live in the `cb:` graph right now; read any of them with `mix bs show`.

- **attestation** - `cb:a386` (kind `observation`): attests what one review session showed about a stale digest file. Grounds in `artifact: user:review-session-2026-04-21` plus one dated evidence entry; `deps=0`. It answers to its source.
- **inference** - `cb:a138` (kind `design-rationale`): concludes that contracts are the abstraction layer between domain expertise and implementation. It grounds in three deps with `artifacts=0 evidence=0 deps=3`; the conclusion is nowhere in the deps individually. That added claim is what could later be shown wrong while all three deps stay true.
- **prescription** - `cb:a489` (kind `convention`): "The graph is the index of obligation: work to do lives as prescriptions - grounded, subject-scoped, conflict-audited, staleness-linked, and queryable." You cannot falsify it; you either follow it or violate it, and the house can withdraw it.
- **contract-grade prescription** - `cb:c051` (kind `state-machine`): the four-types contract itself, with four rules and invariants including "type is a closed enum" and "only prescriptions can carry rules or invariants". A prescription formalized until code can check it.

> **Pitfall.** Do not pick the type by asking "what kind of reasoning did I do?" The verifier never sees your mental act; it sees the relation between the claim, the deps, and the subjects. Author the type the structure will support, then let `mix cb.verify.schema` confirm it. The type follows from the record, and the record is what the verifier reads.

## Immutability: supersede, never edit

A belief is a permanent record. You never open the graph and change a claim that is already there; you supersede it, retract it, or retire it, and the old node stays exactly as written. The founding rule is one attestation, `cb:a302`: beliefs are immutable once created, existing fields are never edited, and the only mutations are status transitions and append-only evidence entries. Everything in this section is a consequence of taking that one sentence seriously.

If a node can never be rewritten, it must be able to leave service some other way. That exit is the status lifecycle, and it is not a convention buried in prose - it is a state-machine contract you can query, `cb:c053`:

```
                     superseded_by
              .------------------------->  superseded   [terminal]
              |
   .--------. |     retracted_on +
   | active |-+---- retracted_reason ---->  retracted    [terminal]
   '--------' |
              |     (prescription only,
              '----- no linkage required -> retired      [terminal]
```

| Transition | What it records | Required linkage |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `active -> superseded` | a better node now stands in its place | `superseded_by` (the successor id) |
| `active -> retracted` | the claim should not stand (wrong or moot) | `retracted_on` + `retracted_reason` |
| `active -> retired` | a standing rule the house stops backing | nothing (prescription-only) |

All non-active states are terminal, and each requires its linkage fields - `superseded_by` is non-null *iff* status is superseded, so the chain can never silently break. **Supersession** means replaced by a named successor: the old claim was fine for its moment, and a sharper or corrected node now does its job; the forward-linked chain is walkable with `mix bs history`. **Retraction** means withdrawn as wrong or moot: no successor, just a date and a reason. **Retired** is the prescription-only exit, and the restriction is worth deriving rather than memorizing: a description was never "in force," so it cannot be withdrawn as a rule - when a description stops matching the world it is *falsified*, which routes to supersession or retraction. Only a prescription can be retired. That is direction of fit (`cb:a471`) surfacing again inside the lifecycle.

Note the contract's own deps: `cb:c053` rests on `cb:a302` (immutability) and `cb:a471` (direction of fit). The lifecycle is grounded in the very ideas it operationalizes - the first hint of a theme chapter 2 completes: in CB, even the foundations are ordinary graph nodes.

**Deprecation is a pause, not an exit.** There is a fourth move that looks like an exit and is gentler: recording the judgment "not currently useful enough to carry" (`cb:a390`). Retraction says the claim is wrong and closes the door; deprecation says the feature failed to earn its keep at the current scale, and the door stays open. Crucially, reinstatement is itself a belief - reversing a deprecation means superseding the deprecation record, so even the reversal leaves a trace. The reference case is the confidence field, below.

**The deliberately mutable exceptions.** Immutability would be useless as a slogan if it had silent exceptions, so CB names them. An active node has exactly three growth points, each additive rather than destructive:

1. **Appending evidence** - the one sanctioned in-place growth point on an immutable node (`cb:a302`, `cb:a522`), through the front door `mix cb.evidence`. A new dated `{date, detail, artifact}` entry joins the `evidence` list; the claim text never moves. You are accumulating more grounding for the same proposition, never restating it.
2. **The materialized link** - a prescription records that its action was carried out by filling `materialized` with `{date, tasks}` (`cb:a380`, `cb:a383`). Whether an action ran is orthogonal to whether the claim holds, so this field tracks execution history without touching truth status.
3. **The status-transition linkage** - when a node leaves `active`, the transition fields are set exactly once. That single write *is* the supersession or retraction.

> **Caveat.** "Mutable" here means append-only. None of the three rewrites a value that was already there. If you find yourself wanting to change the text of a `claim`, that is the signal to author a successor and supersede, not to edit.

**Why the discipline pays: drift becomes detectable.** When a node is superseded, every belief still depending on it is now leaning on something that has been replaced. That is [staleness](../glossary.md#staleness), and the graph computes it deterministically: `mix bs stale` lists beliefs whose deps were superseded or retracted, `--cascade` follows it transitively. An in-place edit would have produced none of this - the old claim would be gone, no chain would exist, and nothing would record that anything changed. Drift stops being something a human has to remember to check and becomes a query that either returns rows or does not. ([Chapter 3](3-operations.md#staleness-detected-not-remembered) operates this machinery.)

**No maintenance hatch.** The strongest version of the argument is `cb:a498`: framework canon changes only through its own write flow, even at the foundations. The proof is that the foundations supersede themselves through the very mechanisms they define: the original three-value type contract `cb:c026` was superseded by the four-value `cb:c051` through ordinary adjudication, and the status-lifecycle contract's own history (`mix bs history cb:c053`) shows the same move. Audit-trail completeness is a property of the structure, not of anyone's behavior: because there is no door that skips the trail, a reviewer never has to ask "did someone quietly hand-fix this contract?"

The 2026-07-01 type rename shows the other side of the same coin: a **vocabulary migration** relabels the enum values while every node keeps its identity and claims - so it is executed as one recorded pass with an evidence entry on the governing contract, not as supersession (evidence 3 on `cb:c051`; the deliberation record is `beliefs/nursery/structural-type-rename.md`). Supersession is for a change of *content*; a label-only migration leaves the identity chain untouched and documents itself in evidence. Both paths leave a trace; neither is a hand-edit.

> **Pitfall.** Hand-editing `beliefs/beliefs.json` to "fix" a node looks like the fast path and quietly destroys both guarantees. It erases the trace - no successor, no date, no reason - and it silently breaks staleness detection, because dependents keep pointing at a node whose meaning shifted underneath them. The repaired file even verifies clean, which is the trap: the verifier checks the current shape and cannot see the history you deleted. Authoring a successor through the write flow is slower by one command and keeps every guarantee on this page intact.

## No confidence scores

Most belief systems attach a number to each claim: a confidence, a probability, a 0-to-1 score. CB attaches none. The governing belief is `cb:a448`: *there are no confidence scores; subjective scores synthesized without a deterministic basis do no load-bearing work; rely on specific evidence and dependency structure instead.*

The argument is worth deriving, because it tells you when the rule applies elsewhere. A confidence score is a subjective scalar: when an author writes `0.7` next to a claim, nothing determines that value - re-run the same author on the same inputs and you might get `0.6` or `0.8`. A quantity with no deterministic basis does no load-bearing work, because no later reader can recompute it, compare it across claims, or act on it without re-importing the guesswork that produced it. Worse, the number hides what it pretends to measure: "0.7 confident" collapses a thin one-source claim and a heavily corroborated finding onto the same scale, erasing the very thing a reader needs to see.

> **Key idea.** A confidence number summarizes the support for a claim into a digit and throws the support away. CB keeps the support and drops the digit: how strongly a claim is held is something you read off its structure, not someone's synthesized guess.

What replaces it is **structural support**: deterministic counts of what actually holds the claim up. `CB.Belief.support/1` returns three integers, nothing synthesized - how many provenance artifacts the belief cites, how many dated evidence entries ground it, and how many deps it stands on. They print as the `Support:` line on every record the shell shows, and because they derive from the node's own fields they are reproducible. Three live records tell the story a score would have hidden:

```
cb:a448   Support: artifacts=1 evidence=1 deps=0   a thin, single-source claim
cb:a138   Support: artifacts=0 evidence=0 deps=3   held up entirely by derivation
cb:c053   Support: artifacts=2 evidence=2 deps=2   grounded through all three channels
```

A reader can rank these without consulting anyone's opinion, and the structure is queryable - every node grounded in a single source, every inference standing on three or more deps. No number gives you that. The confidence field itself was *deprecated*, not erased: `cb:a390` records the reasoning and keeps one path open - a **derived** confidence, computed deterministically from structure, if a concrete use-case ever materializes. What stays rejected is the hand-asserted scalar.

**Expressing uncertainty without a number.** The honest question behind a confidence score is usually real: you are unsure and want to say so. CB's answer is to record the specific shape of the uncertainty in a place that stays actionable:

- Unsure because grounding is thin? **Add evidence** (`mix cb.evidence`) - the support counts rise and the next reader sees more of what the claim stands on.
- Unsure whether a generalization holds? **Author it as an inference** - the uncertainty now lives as a falsifiable node that staleness and supersession can act on.
- Is the uncertainty really "someone should go check"? **Record a prescription** - the doubt lands on the desk as open work ([chapter 3](3-operations.md#obligation-lives-in-the-graph)) instead of sitting as a low number nobody acts on.

> **Pitfall.** The 0-to-1 number feels like information and carries almost none: it cannot be recomputed, cannot be compared across authors, and quietly merges a one-source guess with a corroborated finding. Worse, it discourages the work that would resolve the doubt, because writing `0.6` is faster than adding evidence or splitting out a falsifiable inference. When you feel the pull, name what is uncertain and route it.

---

Trust in CB comes from structure you can re-check, never from a summary you have to take on faith. Immutability keeps the structure honest over time; the absence of a confidence field keeps it honest in the first place.
